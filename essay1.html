Forensic Justification

Several objections to forensic justification have been posed and the responsibility of answering objections falls to whom articulates the position to which is objected. Thus, Erickson responds to objections about his position of forensic justification. The first objection that Erickson treats is the objection that forensic justification is a legal fiction. This objection takes issue with forensic justification by claiming that forensic justification is an impossibility, a deception on the part of God that cannot be true. This objection denies that righteousness can be imputed to a sinner; instead, people must be counted righteous on their own merits, not the merit of another. Erikson's response is based on the constitutive nature of justification, that through the imputed righteousness of Christ, sinners are made righteous through union with Christ. I like Erikson's response to this objection and the analogy of marriage that he uses to explain it.

The second objection is that virtue cannot be transferred from one party to another. Erickson's answer to this simple objection involves the believer's union with Christ once again. Within this union, the spiritual assets and liabilities of the believer, which means that God looks upon the imputed righteousness as the righteousness of Christ, not merely as a transferred righteousness. This interfaces with an understanding of justification as a gift that is seen in the Pauline writings. I would agree with Erickson's argumentation. I se justification as the righteousness of Christ forensically viewed on our behalf through union with Christ.

The third objection that Erickson addresses is that posed by the proponents of the New Perspective on Paul. This objection sees the forensic view of justification as a fundamental misunderstanding of the Pauline materials. Instead of Paul coming to Judaism as a legalistic approach to meriting salvation, Paul is dialoging with Judaizers who insist that Paul's Gentile converts should be circumcised. In the NPP, Judaism was viewed as a religion of a gracious response to God's covenant, and law-keeping was an outflow of grace. The objection was that justification was a contextualized teaching that was situation specific. Erickson addresses this by surveying the biblical evidence that demonstrates the doctrine being emphasized beyond the Judaizers' controversy.

A final objection is the debate between imputed and imparted righteousness in Protestant circles. This position blends justification and sanctification, using Romans 2 to assert that the final judgement is based on works. Erickson's response is that the classic Protestant view is not inadequate, but proper emphasis must be placed on life transformation and that justification and sanctification are distinct, but they always exist together. I concur with Erickson and agree that the Jewish/Western distinction that is emphasized by NPP scholars is highly dubious.
